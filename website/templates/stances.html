The Pugilist's Stance

Conventional Guard

The most popular stance used in the modern era of boxing, the boxer is oriented more square than a fighter in the shell but more bladed than a fighter in a peek-a-boo or high guard. Their hands are oriented by their chin or cheeks and often transition between a long guard or high guard. This stance is well rounded for defense or offense and can be used at any range.
Popular boxers in this stance include: Keith Thurman, Terrence Crawford, Jermall Charlo

Cross Guard/Crab Guard

A rarely used stance where the boxer has both arms crossed, their right hand generally by their left ear, elbow/forearm facing their opponent and their left hand by their liver and their elbow/forearm is also facing their opponent. It’s a very safe stance where the only opening is on the left side of the body, near the liver. The boxer can parry or catch punches on his hands, elbows or forearms. While it’s hard to build an offense off this stance, it’s excellent for defense, can be used to close the distance with little to no damage or on the way out from close range. Requires excellent use of footwork, bobbing, weaving and rolling to get on the inside to counter/pot shot or wrap your opponent up. Forearms and elbows are used liberally to frame the opponent’s head/chin and smother their offense by breaking their stance and giving them little room to work.
Popular boxers in this stance include: Archie Moore, Ken Norton, Evander Holyfield, Henry Armstrong

High Guard/Double Guard

A popular boxing stance that is generally most effective at mid to close range. Perfect for the Catch-and-shoot style of boxing where the boxer catches punches on his guard and fires his own punch in return. This stance is mostly used by Mexican fighters who are known for their aggression and propensity to fight on the inside, catching and trading punches till one fighter drops.
Popular boxers in this stance include: Julio César Chavez, Gervonta 'Tank' Davis and Saul 'Canelo' Alvarez.

Long-Guard/Half-Guard

The long guard is a stance where the rear hand is held high by the temple and the lead hand is held up and out to provide traffic for incoming punches. The lead elbow can be used to deflect and block punches as well, an excellent guard for long rangy fighters. A variation that's rarely used in current boxing is called the Mummy Guard. More often used in Muay Thai, both hands are held up and out, ready to catch or parry incoming blows, it's used moreso in spots rather than a full stance. George Foreman used this guard to control his opponent's head and shoulders, using his immense strength to keep his opponents on the outside. Larry Holmes also used this guard in spots to catch and deflect incoming punches.
Popular boxers in this stance include: Larry Holmes, Wladimir Klitschko, Lennox Lewis, Anthony Joshua

Low-Guard

Like the name implies, the boxer keeps both hands in a low position. Allows for easier upper body mobility to sway, pull and slip punches as well as counter their opponent from a low angle. The stance inherently works as a way to bait punches and confuse your opponent.
Popular boxers in this stance include: Roy Jones Jr., 'Prince' Naseem Hamed, Pernell 'Sweet Pea' Whittaker

Peek-a-boo/Tight Defense

Contrary to popular belief the peek-a-boo defense is a stance, not a style. Both hands are held tightly in front of the opponent's face and his torso is oriented in a squared position to his opponent. Popularized by Cus D'amato and his protégés Floyd Patterson and Mike Tyson, the peek-a-boo is a stance to be used for aggressive counter punching, generally the counters come after the punch, depending on the peek-a-boo user to avoid and catch straight punches and fire back with hooks and uppercuts. This stance can also be used by sluggers and boxer punchers namely, Ike Quartey and Jose Torres, where the squared torso allows for more powerful jabs and provides strong defense in mid to long range because it doesn’t allow straight punches to split the guard.
Popular boxers in this stance include: Mike Tyson, Floyd Patterson, Ike Quartey

Philly Shell/Detroit Shell/Stonewall Defense

Variation of Cross-Guard developed in Detroit, Michigan. Popularized by Floyd Mayweather, it’s generally used by counter punchers effective in close and mid/long range. Lead hand is held low and across the belly, lead shoulder is the first line of defense protecting the chin and used to roll straights/crosses. Rear hand can be held high or lower by the jaw, used to parry and catch jabs/hooks. Torso is generally oriented in a bladed stance but can be squared toward the opponent.
Popular boxers in this stance include: George Benton, Floyd Mayweather, Ken Norton

Southpaw

The mirror image of most right-handed fighters, southpaws have an innate advantage due to how rare they are and often find success in frustrating and outmaneuvering their opponents.
Popular boxers in this stance include: Ronald 'Winky' Wright, Vasily Lomanchenko and Terrence Crawford*
